"Warmer": An infographic about Carbon Dioxide (CO2) emmisions and climate change

A global problem:


The global production of CO2 is a significant contributer to the greenhouse effect.


Released from the burning of fossil fuels, Carbon Dioxide (often shortened to its chemical formula, CO2) is a greenhouse gas which is slowly heating up the Earth's average temperature.


This has a wide range of detrimental effects, from widespread habitat destruction to sea level rise, and if the release of CO2 is not curbed in time the effects could become irreversible.

A worsening problem:


The global production of CO2 has been increasing drastically, year after year, at an unsustainable rate.


CO2 release has come hand in hand with economic development, as countries burned more and more fossil fuels to meet their growing energy needs.


This is coupled with increases in the rate of release of other greenhouse gasses such as Methane from agricultural development.


As shown to the right, the rate has increased exponentially over the last few centuries, from the onset of the industrial age in the 1800s, to the significant automation of industries in the 1900s.

A disproportionate challenge:


The largest countries in the world produce the most CO2, but it is often the smallest ones that will face the heaviest consequences.


The largest countries in the world have the most resources to adjust to the effects of climate change, with immense finances and other resources allowing them to seek out alternatives in a changing world.


This is not the same for smaller countries however, especially those that are considered developing nations. These countrie will most likely face severe adversity, especially if they lie along the equator where the warming of global temperatures would be felt the most.


Increases in adverse weather such as droughts and monsoons will be difficult, if not impossible for small nations to properly overcome.
